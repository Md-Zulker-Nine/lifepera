10 Cultural Mistakes Tourists Make in Thailand

Zulker Nine • July 24, 2026 • LifePera Blog
🇹🇭
Advertisement • 728×90

Thailand is legendary for a reason. Between the chaotic street food stalls of Bangkok, the misty jungle peaks of Chiang Mai, and the turquoise waters of the southern islands, it is one of the most welcoming destinations on Earth. Locals are famous for their easy smiles, and the overall vibe is overwhelmingly relaxed. But that legendary hospitality sometimes tricks travelers into thinking anything goes. It doesn't.

Beneath the surface of tropical relaxation lies a deeply traditional society built on respect, social hierarchy, and Buddhism. When you are traveling in Thailand, it is shockingly easy to commit a cultural faux pas without even realizing it. To help you travel smarter, respect local customs, and avoid awkward moments, here are 10 common cultural mistakes tourists make in Thailand—and how to avoid them.

1. Disrespecting the Monarchy

In Thailand, the royal family is treated with profound reverence. This is not just a social expectation; it is legally enforced. Thailand has some of the strictest lese-majesty laws in the world. Speaking critically of the king, queen, or royal family can result in massive fines or severe prison sentences—and foreigners are not exempt.

The Fix: Never make jokes about the monarchy, deface Thai currency (which features the king's portrait), or express negative opinions about royals in public. When the national anthem plays in public spaces—such as train stations at 8:00 AM and 6:00 PM—stop moving and observe, just like the locals do.

2. Touching Someone’s Head or Pointing with Your Feet

In Buddhist philosophy, the head is considered the most sacred part of the human body, while the feet are the lowest, dirtiest part. Unfortunately, tourists constantly violate this hierarchy. Ruffling a child's hair out of affection or accidentally brushing someone with your foot can cause instant offense.

The Fix: Keep your hands to yourself when it comes to people's heads. When sitting on the floor, tuck your feet away from monks, elders, and Buddha statues. Never point your feet at a person or a sacred object, and definitely do not use your foot to nudge a dropped item toward someone.

3. Dressing Inappropriately at Temples

It is hot and humid in Thailand, which makes wearing minimal clothing tempting. However, wandering into a sacred Buddhist temple in a tank top, crop top, or short shorts is considered deeply disrespectful.

The Fix: Both men and women should cover their shoulders and knees when visiting temples. Pack a lightweight sarong or a pair of breathable linen pants in your day bag so you can easily slip them on before entering temple grounds. Always take off your shoes before stepping inside the main prayer halls.

4. Losing Your Cool in Public

Thailand is known as the "Land of Smiles," and for good reason: social harmony is prized above all else. Raising your voice, arguing aggressively, or losing your temper in public—known as "losing face"—is considered immature and counterproductive. If you yell at a hotel receptionist or a street vendor, they won't try harder to help you; they will simply shut down and lose respect for you.

The Fix: Keep your cool, no matter how frustrating a situation gets. Smile, speak softly, and treat people with patience. A calm, polite demeanor will get you far better results than a tantrum every single time.

5. Treating Monks Like Tourist Attractions

Buddhist monks in their bright orange robes are an iconic sight across Thailand, and you will naturally want to take photos. However, monks are not props for your vacation scrapbook, and women face strict boundaries regarding them.

The Fix: Always ask permission before taking a photo of a monk. Women must be especially careful never to touch a monk or hand anything directly to them. If a female traveler needs to give an offering or an alms item to a monk, she must place it on a cloth on the table rather than handing it to him directly.

6. Ignoring the Wai

The wai—the traditional greeting where you press your palms together in a prayer-like gesture with a slight bow—is a fundamental part of Thai etiquette. Tourists often ignore it, wave casually, or try to use it with everyone they meet.

The Fix: You don't need to wai everyone (you don't need to use it with service staff, street vendors, or children). However, when an elder, a business partner, or someone of higher status greets you with a wai, it is polite to return it. As a general rule, tourists should wait for locals to initiate the gesture.

7. Buying Souvenirs Made from Wildlife

Walking through night markets in Thailand, you might stumble across stalls selling ivory trinkets, dried seahorses, snake wine, or products made from turtle shells and coral. While these might seem like unique travel mementos, buying them supports illegal wildlife trafficking and environmental destruction.

The Fix: Vote with your wallet and skip animal-product souvenirs entirely. If you want a meaningful keepsake, opt for locally made textiles, hand-carved wooden items, or artisanal coffee from northern hill tribes.

8. Riding Elephants and Visiting Unethical Sanctuaries

For decades, elephant riding was marketed as a quintessential Thai adventure. Thankfully, awareness has shifted, but animal tourism is still a massive trap. Many places still use the label "sanctuary" while secretly breaking elephants' spirits through cruel training methods.

The Fix: Do your research before booking an animal experience. True sanctuaries do not allow riding, bathing with elephants, or circus-style tricks. Choose ethical operations where you can simply observe these majestic animals roaming freely from a respectful distance.

9. Failing to Tip or Tipping Excessively

Tipping is not traditionally part of Thai culture, but the massive influx of Western tourism has changed expectations in major tourist hubs. On the flip side, leaving huge, extravagant tips in small family-run eateries can sometimes confuse or embarrass the staff.

The Fix: At local street food stalls and casual neighborhood joints, tipping isn't expected at all. In sit-down restaurants with service charges, rounding up the bill or leaving a modest cash tip (around 20 to 50 baht) is greatly appreciated. In high-end establishments, 10% is standard.

10. Overlooking Local Dining Etiquette

Thai food is meant to be shared. Ordering an entire plate of food just for yourself while everyone else at the table is sharing dishes from communal platters breaks the rhythm of a traditional meal.

The Fix: Order a few different dishes to share with your table—typically including a mix of curry, soup, stir-fry, and rice. Use your serving spoon (not your personal eating spoon) to scoop food from the communal plates onto your own plate. And remember: unless you are eating noodle soup, use your fork to push food onto your spoon, rather than eating directly with the fork.

Traveling responsibly in Thailand is all about paying attention to the details and respecting local traditions. By keeping these cultural nuances in mind, you will not only avoid awkward situations but also build deeper, more authentic connections with the people you meet.
